K-State Enrollment Statistics Over The Past 10 Years

Natalie Leonard | For MC 316

December 5, 2020

The fall of 2020 has not been easy on anyone. As the world grapples to maintain a sense of normality amidst the COVID-19 pandemic, it has been made clear that no one will escape this year unaffected. Students and universities are no exception. This fall, Kansas State University’s enrollment numbers are down nearly 4%. Across all of its campuses, the university is teaching 865 less students, as stated in the preliminary Kansas Board of Regents campus statistics report, making this semester the lowest in terms of enrollment since 1998. Although the pandemic has accelerated the issue, the coronavirus is not to blame for the past 10 years of a steady decline in students choosing to attend the university. After analyzing the university’s enrollment data from years past, interviewing K-State’s enrollment professionals and speaking with both students who returned to K-State this semester and others who chose to take time off, it is clear there are many factors at play when students decide where to invest in their college education.

14% Decline Within The Last 5 Years

Looking back over K-State’s last ten years, the university has had its ups and downs in terms of attendance. The fall of 2014 saw the highest number in K-State history, with 24,766 students enrolled. Within the last five years, the total enrollment has declined nearly 14%, the largest decline in any state university in Kansas.

Impact of COVID-19

Surveying the data, it can be observed that COVID-19 cannot be held totally responsible for the decline in K-State’s enrollment, however the pandemic has done some considerable damage. Lauren Schath was a junior at K-State last semester who had fully intended on returning to Manhattan in the fall to finish out her senior year and graduate with a degree in elementary education. However, everything changed once COVID-19 struck the united states in the early spring of this year. With many classes only offered online and all of the uncertainties about when life will return to a state of normalcy, Schath made the tough decision to not return to K-State this semester.

“I did not want to pay all that money for an education when I did not feel I was getting everything I could out of it,” Schath said. She explained that she did not take this decision lightly, but that on her time spent at home she has been able to earn some money while working as a para at an elementary school in her hometown- a job that she says she has loved. She looks forward to her plans of returning to K-State next August.

For students who chose to return to K-State, this semester has been unlike any other. Senior in dietetics, Jordyn Granger, decided to reenroll at K-State this semester in order to keep her job in Manhattan and to stay on track for graduation in May. Despite her excitement to return to K-State, she says this school year has not been easy.

“School is online, and I have zero motivation,” Granger said. “I feel like I’m not getting the education I got in past semesters because I was more engaged in-person. I feel like I have to teach myself.” Granger said that if given the chance to go back and redecide whether to attend K-State this semester or not, she “probably would’ve taken time off to spend time with family and to work.”

Given the circumstances, it is no wonder that so many students have chosen to sit this semester out from college, but unfortunately the lack of tuition funding is being felt within the university.

Effects of Enrollment Decrease

This year, 40% of K-State’s budget plan was expected to come from tuition. The remaining 60% was generated from alternative means, a major contributor included funding from the state. However, within the last 10 years, Kansas has reduced this funding significantly. According to Ethan Erickson, K-State’s Chief Financial Officer and Director of Budget Planning, the university has had to reduce this year’s budget dramatically to accommodate for funding fluctuations due to state cuts and COVID-19, forcing the university to make difficult decisions.

“Eighty-eight percent of our budget is tied up in people,” Erickson said, “and so when you reduce the budget, you have got to reduce your personnel salaries as well. That’s the really big impact that happens when both enrollment and state funding go down.”

Although the university is looking for alternative ways to cut the budget, such as eliminating travel expenses during the pandemic, letting faculty go has unfortunately had to become a reality. Erickson said that he can’t think of a department at K-State that hasn’t been impacted by these budget cuts in some fashion. Looking Towards The Future

Looking at years to come, K-State, along with the rest of the world, is hopeful for a soon end to the pandemic that has done so much damage.

“The real pessimistic side of me would say, we’ll never look like we did pre-COVID,” Erickson said, speaking about the K-State budget.

However, looking at past numbers, it’s clear that K-State is going to have to correct pre-existing issues if the university wants to see a rise in enrollment. According to Erickson, the pandemic may have created a steeper decline in enrollment numbers at K-State, but it was just accelerating trends the university has been seeing for the past 10 years.

Data collection and data analysis

This data was collected from K-State's website. The data was then analyzed in a google spreadsheet, where it was cleaned. I also spoke with Dr. Bin Ning, the Associate Provost of Institutional Research at K-State who provided the enrollment numbers for 2020. I cross checked these numbers with K-State’s Office of the Registrar to ensure they were correct. Additional sources include the Collegian's article, K-State’s overall enrollment falls 4.1 percent in fall 2020, and interviews with Ethan Erickson, Jordyn Granger and Lauren Schath.
